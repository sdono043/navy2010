NAMED FOR

Fleet Admiral Chester W. Nimitz

FEBRUARY 24, 1885 – FEBRUARY 20, 1966

USNA Class of 1905  ·  Commander in Chief, U.S. Pacific Fleet, 1941–1945

THE NAMESAKE

Chester Nimitz was born in Fredericksburg, Texas, in the German Hill Country, the grandson of a seafaring merchant captain from Bremen who filled young Chester with sea stories. Nimitz applied to West Point but was told there were no vacancies. Learning of an opening at the Naval Academy, he pursued it with equal determination and graduated in 1905, seventh in a class of 114.

His early career was marked by an incident that would have ended lesser officers: as commanding officer of the destroyer USS Decatur in 1908, he ran the ship aground and was court-martialed and formally reprimanded. He recovered, became an expert submariner and diesel engineer, and rose through the interwar Navy as a respected commander and administrator.

On December 31, 1941 — three weeks after the attack on Pearl Harbor — Nimitz stood on the deck of the submarine USS Grayling in Pearl Harbor and, in a ceremony of unusual urgency, assumed command of the shattered U.S. Pacific Fleet. What he inherited was a devastated force, a broken base, and a nation in shock. What he built over the next three and a half years was the most sweeping naval campaign in human history.

Nimitz orchestrated the pivotal Battle of Midway in June 1942 — the turning point of the Pacific War — followed by Guadalcanal, the island-hopping campaign across the Central Pacific, the Battle of the Philippine Sea, Leyte Gulf, Iwo Jima, and Okinawa. He promoted bold commanders like Raymond Spruance and William "Bull" Halsey, managed enormous coalition logistics, and maintained calm strategic judgment under conditions of extreme pressure. On September 2, 1945, he signed Japan's instrument of surrender aboard USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay on behalf of the United States.

THE LIBRARY

In 1944 Nimitz became one of only four men in U.S. history to hold the five-star rank of Fleet Admiral. After the war he served as Chief of Naval Operations before retiring in 1947. He died in 1966 and is buried at Golden Gate National Cemetery in San Francisco. The Nimitz Library, completed in 1973 and designed by architect John Carl Warnecke, replaced Mahan Hall as the Academy's main library. It houses the vast academic and historical research collections the Naval Academy requires — including the Chester W. Nimitz Papers.
