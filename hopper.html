NAMED FOR

Rear Admiral Grace Brewster Murray Hopper

DECEMBER 9, 1906 – JANUARY 1, 1992

Mathematician · Computer Scientist · U.S. Navy Officer · Pioneer of Programming

THE NAMESAKE

Grace Murray was born in New York City and showed early aptitude in mathematics and science. She graduated Phi Beta Kappa from Vassar College in 1928, earned a master's degree from Yale in 1930, and a Ph.D. in mathematics from Yale in 1934 — one of the relatively few women to earn a mathematics doctorate at a major American university in that era. She joined the faculty at Vassar as an associate professor.

When the United States entered World War II, Hopper left her faculty position and joined the Navy WAVES (Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service) in 1943. She was assigned to the Bureau of Ships Computation Project at Harvard, where she became one of the first three programmers on the Harvard Mark I — one of the earliest electromechanical computers — working under Howard Aiken. It was during this period that she reportedly encountered the moth trapped in a relay that would make her famous: the team taped it into the logbook, and Hopper popularized the term "debugging" for removing errors from computer systems.

After the war, Hopper stayed in naval service and at Remington Rand developed the first compiler — a program that translates human-readable instructions into machine code. This was a breakthrough that made programming vastly more accessible. She championed machine-independent programming languages at a time when most engineers believed computers could only understand mathematics. This philosophy drove the development of FLOW-MATIC and ultimately COBOL (Common Business-Oriented Language), which she helped create through the CODASYL consortium in 1959. COBOL became one of the most widely used programming languages in history and remains in use today, processing an enormous share of global financial transactions.

"It's easier to ask forgiveness than to get permission."

Hopper retired from the Navy in 1986 at age 79 — the oldest serving officer in the U.S. armed forces — holding the rank of Rear Admiral. She was known for distributing "nanoseconds" to explain computing speeds: a length of wire approximately 11.8 inches long representing the distance light travels in one nanosecond. Admiral Hopper received the Defense Distinguished Service Medal and, posthumously, the Presidential Medal of Freedom.

THE BUILDING

First building at any US military service academy dedicated to cybersecurity
First academic building at a major US service academy named for a woman

The Grace Hopper Center for Cyber Security Studies is a 206,400-square-foot facility designed by Skidmore, Owings & Merrill (SOM) and built by Turner Construction at a cost of approximately $143 million. It opened on October 15, 2020 and houses six academic majors: Cyber Operations, Computer Engineering, Computer Science, Electrical Engineering, Information Technology, and Robotics and Control Engineering. Facilities include an anechoic chamber, green energy lab, optics lab, and power studio. It represents USNA's commitment to leading the nation in cybersecurity education — at a moment when cyber has become the fifth domain of warfare.
