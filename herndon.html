Herndon Monument

ERECTED 1860  ·  HERNDON CLIMB SINCE 1940

NAMED FOR

Commander William Lewis Herndon

OCTOBER 25, 1813 – SEPTEMBER 12, 1857

U.S. Navy Officer · Explorer · Went down with his ship, SS Central America

THE NAMESAKE

William Lewis Herndon was born in Fredericksburg, Virginia, and entered the U.S. Navy as a midshipman at age 15. He proved to be an able and adventurous officer, rising to the rank of Commander over three decades of sea service. In 1851 the Navy Department assigned him to lead an exploration of the Valley of the Amazon River in South America, descending from the Andes to the Atlantic Ocean. His published report, Exploration of the Valley of the Amazon (1854), became a widely read document of geographic science.

The report had an unexpected cultural impact: a young man named Sam Clemens read Herndon's account and was captivated by the romantic possibilities of South American rivers. The account inspired his passion for riverboat life on the Mississippi — the life he later immortalized under the pen name Mark Twain. Herndon's Amazon journey, in a roundabout way, gave American literature one of its defining voices.

By 1857 Herndon was in command of the SS Central America, a 272-foot sidewheel steamer operated on the Panama-to-New York route. On September 8, 1857, as the ship steamed northward through the Atlantic carrying 477 passengers — many of them California gold miners returning home with their fortunes — she sailed into a Category 2 hurricane off the coast of North Carolina.

Over three days the storm battered the ship, flooding her hold and disabling her engines. Herndon organized the passengers and crew with remarkable calm, managed to signal a passing brig, and on the afternoon of September 12 transferred 153 women and children to safety. With no prospect of saving the ship, Herndon refused to leave her. He dressed in his full-dress uniform, took his position on the paddlebox, and as SS Central America sank beneath the Atlantic that evening, he went down with her and the approximately 425 men who remained aboard. He was 43 years old. Herndon was mourned nationwide as a hero. The officers of the Naval Academy Class of 1860 commissioned the 21-foot granite obelisk that now stands in his name.

THE HERNDON CLIMB

The Herndon Climb is one of the most iconic rites of passage in American military education. On the day of the climb, ~50 pounds of vegetable shortening are applied to the monument; the entire plebe class removes their shoes and works together — building a human pyramid — until one member reaches the top, replaces the plebe "dixie cup" cover with an officer's cover, and plebe year is officially over.

1940

The Herndon Climb tradition begins as a simple end-of-plebe-year ceremony.

1947

An upperclassman's cover is placed atop the monument as the symbol plebes must retrieve.

1949

Upperclassmen begin greasing the monument with lard to increase the difficulty.

1962

A plebe "dixie cup" is placed on top before the climb, establishing the full modern ritual.

2006

The Class of 2010 completes their Herndon Climb at the end of plebe year.

Tradition holds — though not officially confirmed — that the midshipman who places the officer's cover is destined to become the first admiral of the class. The record time for the climb is under four minutes. Difficult grease conditions can extend the effort to over three hours. The plebes donate their athletic shoes to charity after the climb — a rite of community service woven into the passage.

KEY FACTS

  • Herndon died September 12, 1857 going down with SS Central America in a hurricane
  • Transferred 153 women and children to safety before refusing to abandon ship
  • His 1854 Amazon exploration account inspired a young Mark Twain
  • 21-foot granite obelisk commissioned by the Naval Academy Class of 1860
  • Herndon Climb tradition began 1940
  • Greasing of monument began 1949
  • Dixie cup on top added 1962 — full modern ritual established
  • Tradition holds the midshipman who places the officer's cover will be the class's first admiral
  • For the Class of 2010, the Herndon Climb took place in spring 2006
