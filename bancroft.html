ACADEMY BUILDING

Bancroft Hall

COMPLETED 1906  ·  WINGS FINISHED 1961

NAMED FOR

George Bancroft

OCTOBER 3, 1800 – JANUARY 17, 1891

Historian · Secretary of the Navy · Founder of the United States Naval Academy

THE NAMESAKE

George Bancroft was one of the most distinguished intellects in 19th-century America. A Massachusetts native, he graduated from Harvard at 17, then earned a Ph.D. from the University of Göttingen in 1820 — studying alongside Georg Hegel and Alexander von Humboldt. He became the foremost American historian of his era, producing the monumental ten-volume History of the United States from the Discovery of the American Continent, a work that took decades to write and made him internationally renowned.

His political loyalty earned him an appointment as Secretary of the Navy under President Polk in 1845 — and that single year of service changed American military history. Bancroft recognized that the system of training officers at sea — chaotic, inconsistent, and often corrupt — had to end. Drawing on the academic plan drafted by his brilliant young faculty member William Chauvenet, he moved quickly and decisively.

On October 10, 1845, Bancroft opened the Naval School at Fort Severn in Annapolis through a clever bureaucratic maneuver: he transferred Fort Severn from Army to Navy jurisdiction and reorganized existing appropriations to fund it — without waiting for Congress. Fifty midshipmen and seven professors began the first term. Congress formally backed the institution the following year. Bancroft went on to serve as Minister to Great Britain, Prussia, and the German Empire, living to age 90 in one of the most productive intellectual careers in American history.

THE BUILDING

Designed by architect Ernest Flagg in the Beaux-Arts style, the central section of Bancroft Hall rose from 1901 to 1906, with the building fully operational for the 1907–1908 academic year. Additional wings followed across six decades: Wings 5 and 6 in 1918–1921, Wings 1 and 2 in 1941 as America prepared for war, and Wings 7 and 8 in 1961, reaching the building's final dimensions.

The result is staggering in scale. Bancroft Hall encompasses 33 acres of floor space — making it the world's largest single college dormitory by a wide margin. Its 1,639 dormitory rooms and 4,663 beds house every midshipman at the Academy under one roof, organized by company. The interconnected corridors, formally called "passageways," stretch 4.8 miles in total length. The building has its own ZIP code: 21412.

Inside the main entrance, Memorial Hall is a sacred space displaying the battle flags of the naval service — among them the "Don't Give Up the Ship" pennant from Commodore Perry's flagship at the Battle of Lake Erie, the most famous pennant in American naval history. For every midshipman, Bancroft Hall is simply "Mother B" or "The Hall" — the place that defines the rhythms of four years.
