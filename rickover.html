ACADEMIC BUILDING · ENGINEERING

Rickover Hall

COMPLETED 1975  ·  USNA CLASS OF 1922

NAMED FOR

Admiral Hyman G. Rickover

JANUARY 27, 1900 – JULY 8, 1986

USNA Class of 1922  ·  "Father of the Nuclear Navy"

THE NAMESAKE

Hyman Rickover was born in Makow, Russian Poland, the son of a Jewish tailor who brought his family to Chicago when Rickover was six years old. He received an appointment to the Naval Academy and graduated in the Class of 1922 — not a standout midshipman, but possessed of an intense, analytical mind that would eventually reshape American military power. After World War II, during which he oversaw ship repair in the Pacific with characteristic demanding precision, Rickover was selected to explore nuclear propulsion for the Navy.

In 1948, over significant institutional opposition, he was assigned to head the joint Navy-Atomic Energy Commission naval nuclear propulsion program. What followed was one of the most consequential engineering achievements in American military history.

Rickover drove the development of the world's first nuclear-powered vessels — USS Nautilus (SSN-571), launched in 1954 and commissioned in 1955, and USS Seawolf (SSN-575). When Nautilus sent the message "Underway on nuclear power" in January 1955, it was a signal not just of technological achievement but of strategic transformation. Nuclear propulsion freed submarines from their dependence on surface air and allowed them to remain submerged for weeks at a time, fundamentally altering naval warfare.

"Underway on nuclear power." — USS Nautilus, January 17, 1955

Rickover served as Director of Naval Reactors for over three decades, maintaining obsessive personal control over every nuclear-trained officer and every reactor system in the fleet. His notoriously rigorous personal interviews for nuclear submarine duty — designed to probe character and judgment under stress — became legendary. Future President Jimmy Carter, a naval officer who served under the nuclear program, described the interview as among the most demanding experiences of his life. Rickover served as a flag officer for nearly 30 years, an extraordinary tenure achieved only through repeated Congressional intervention overriding Navy retirement policies.

THE BUILDING

Under Rickover's leadership, more than 150 U.S. naval vessels were brought under nuclear power, accumulating over 3,000 ship-years of accident-free reactor operations — a safety record of extraordinary precision. Rickover Hall, completed in 1975, today houses USNA's Departments of Mechanical Engineering, Naval Architecture, Ocean Engineering, and Aeronautical and Aerospace Engineering. It is fitting that the most technically demanding building at the Academy bears the name of the officer who made technical exactness a matter of national security.
