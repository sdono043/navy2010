Tripoli Monument

CARVED 1806  ·  AT USNA SINCE 1860

HONORING

Six Officers of the First Barbary War

KILLED IN ACTION · 1804–1805

America's first overseas military conflict · The birth of US naval power projection

THE MONUMENT

The Tripoli Monument stands as the oldest military monument in the United States — predating the Washington Monument by nearly 40 years — and holds the further distinction of being the first public monument ever erected in Washington, D.C. For 35 years it was the only monument in the Federal capital. Its origins lie in the First Barbary War (1801–1805), America's first overseas military conflict, fought against the pirate states of North Africa that had been extorting tribute from American merchant ships.

President Thomas Jefferson refused to continue paying. He sent a naval squadron to enforce American rights on the sea. The resulting campaign included several dramatic actions, including Lieutenant Stephen Decatur's audacious midnight raid to burn USS Philadelphia — captured by Tripoli — in Tripoli Harbor in 1804. Lord Horatio Nelson reportedly called it "the most bold and daring act of the Age."

THE SIX HONORED

Master Commandant Richard Somers
Lieutenant James Caldwell
James Decatur (brother of Stephen)
Lieutenant Henry Wadsworth
Lieutenant Joseph Israel
Midshipman John Sword Dorsey

Among them: Commander Richard Somers and most of his crew died on September 4, 1804 when the fire ship USS Intrepid — loaded with explosives and intended to be sailed into Tripoli Harbor and detonated — exploded prematurely, killing all aboard. Henry Wadsworth was the great-uncle of poet Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, who was named for him.

THE JOURNEY OF THE MONUMENT

At the war's conclusion, Navy Captain David Porter — who would later become famous in the War of 1812 — undertook the task of commissioning a fitting monument. Working with the Bishop of Florence, Porter engaged the Livorno sculptor Micali, who carved the white marble column at half his normal fee ($3,000) as a contribution to American honor. The finished monument — a 30-foot column topped by an American eagle, its base adorned with allegorical figures of Glory, Fame, History, and Commerce — was shipped to America aboard none other than USS Constitution ("Old Ironsides") and installed at the Washington Navy Yard in 1808.

1806Carved in Livorno, Italy by sculptor Micali
1808Transported to America aboard USS Constitution; installed at Washington Navy Yard
1831Moved to the west terrace of the US Capitol as Washington grew
1860Transferred to the Naval Academy in Annapolis — its permanent home

For midshipmen, the Tripoli Monument represents the founding sacrifice of American naval officers — men who died in the first war the young republic chose to fight, rather than pay ransom. It stands on Stribling Walk as a daily reminder that the freedom of the seas has never been free.

KEY FACTS

  • Oldest military monument in the United States
  • First public monument erected in Washington, D.C.
  • For 35 years, the only monument in the Federal capital
  • Carved in Livorno, Italy, 1806; sculptor Micali charged half his usual fee
  • Commissioned by Captain David Porter with help from the Bishop of Florence
  • Transported to America aboard USS Constitution ("Old Ironsides")
  • Honors six officers killed in the First Barbary War (1801–1805)
  • Three homes: Navy Yard 1808 → US Capitol 1831 → USNA 1860
